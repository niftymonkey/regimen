# Feedback is the center; Guidance and Enforcement are the levers acted with

> Status: accepted (2026-06-23)

ADR-0001 framed Regimen as a discipline embodied in pluggable instruments adopted individually, and ADR-0002 named three of them, Guidance, Enforcement, and Feedback, cut by the mechanism each uses on the interaction. Both framings treat the three as co-equal peers distinguished only by mechanism. This ADR supersedes that co-equality. Feedback is not one of three peers; it is the center, the observability that makes the engineer-and-agent interaction inspectable instead of felt. Guidance and Enforcement are the levers the engineer reaches for in response to what Feedback surfaces, the levers Regimen provides today. The structure does not fix the lever count at two: a future lever would be an addition to it, not a contradiction of it, which ADR-0001 already allows for. What ADR-0001 and ADR-0002 got right is preserved: the felt-needs adoption motivation from ADR-0001 (you pick up a lever when a need arises, never a practice adopted wholesale) and the advisory-versus-mandatory reliability boundary from ADR-0002 (Guidance asks, Enforcement compels). What is superseded is only the flat, three-co-equal-instruments cut that sat on top of those. The full mental model this records lives in `../mental-model.md`; this ADR is the decision and its supersede scope.

## Why the co-equal cut never held

The object Regimen acts on is the engineer-and-agent interaction, and the problem it solves is that today that interaction runs on feel: you carry impressions about whether a session went well, why, and what to change, but no data on any of it. Regimen turns that feel into data. That is observability, and observability is Feedback. So Feedback is not a third item beside two others; it is the reason the system exists. The other two earn their place only as responses to what Feedback shows: you see a pattern (Feedback), and you act on it by adjusting a Guidance skill or adding an Enforcement gate. A framing that lists all three as co-equal mechanisms hides that dependency, and in practice it read as a wiring diagram of parts rather than a description of use, which is exactly why it failed to communicate the project.

## The structure is a loop, not a list

The shape is a loop with three beats. See: Feedback surfaces a pattern the engineer would otherwise only feel. Act: the engineer pulls a lever, building or adjusting a Guidance skill or adding an Enforcement gate. Validate: Feedback shows whether the change actually moved anything, which is itself a fresh observation, so the loop closes back to see. Regimen owns the seeing (the first and third beats); the engineer owns the acting (the second). This is the precise sense in which Feedback is the center and Guidance and Enforcement are levers: the center observes, the levers act, and the levers are pulled by the engineer in response to the center. The validate beat is not optional, because a lever is not free: a skill, a config line, a gate, or an added server each carries cost, so an unvalidated lever can be dead weight, and only Feedback can tell whether it earned its keep.

## What survives from ADR-0001 and ADR-0002

The discipline-as-spine idea from ADR-0001 survives, sharpened: the spine is Feedback, the observability that gives the levers their coherence, not an abstract discipline floating above three equal tools. The felt-needs adoption motivation survives unchanged and is now explained by the loop: you reach for a lever when Feedback, or your own felt experience before Feedback is installed, surfaces the need. The mechanism boundary from ADR-0002 survives in full: Guidance is advisory and the model may ignore it, Enforcement is mandatory and removes the choice, and that boundary is now the distinction between two kinds of lever rather than between two of three peers. What does not survive is placing Feedback inside that same mechanism cut, because Feedback is a different kind of thing: it does not act on the interaction, it observes it.

## Considered options

- Keep the three co-equal instruments cut by mechanism (ADR-0002 as written). Rejected: it hides that Feedback is the reason the system exists and that the other two are responses to it, and it reads as a parts diagram rather than a use, which is why it never landed when explaining Regimen.
- Edit ADR-0001 and ADR-0002 in place to demote Feedback's peers. Rejected: ADRs are an immutable decision record; a changed decision is recorded by a superseding ADR, not by rewriting history. ADR-0001 and ADR-0002 carry a short update note pointing to this ADR, and their text otherwise stands as the record of what was decided then.
- Fold Guidance and Enforcement into Feedback as one instrument. Rejected: it conflates seeing with acting. The engineer owns the acting and Regimen owns the seeing; collapsing them would erase the boundary that makes the validate beat meaningful and would deny Guidance and Enforcement the names their distinct mechanisms earn.
- Drop the word instrument entirely and call Guidance and Enforcement only actions. Rejected as unnecessary: they are durable, adoptable things with a real advisory-versus-mandatory boundary worth naming. Lever captures that they are pulled in response to Feedback without discarding the mechanism distinction ADR-0002 drew.

## What this ADR does not change

It does not touch ADR-0003: Feedback still measures the conversation, never the software, and never renders a verdict on the engineer. It does not change ADR-0008's judged-signal schema or rubric, which is the grounding the center reads. It reinforces ADR-0012, which already took the pillar nouns off the command line because a user judging a session should not have to know which instrument owns a verb; this ADR generalizes that stance from the CLI to the whole conceptual model. It does not alter the harness- and model-agnostic constraint, the data and capture architecture, or any harness-invoked script. It changes how the three relate, not what any of them is built from.
